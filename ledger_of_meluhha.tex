\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\enspace
One hundred years of Indus script decipherment has produced over a
hundred proposed readings --- Dravidian, Indo-Aryan, Sumerian, Munda
--- none achieving consensus, because no bilingual exists and
4.6-sign texts underdetermine any phonetic model.
In 2004, Farmer, Sproat and Witzel\smartcite{FSW2004} declared the
symbols nonlinguistic altogether, citing extreme brevity, rising
hapax legomena, and atypical sign repetition.
Both positions share one assumption: the signs are either a spoken
language or not writing at all.
This book proposes a third position.
The Indus Valley script (c.\ 2600--1900~\textsc{bce}) is a
structured metrological code --- a field-delimited cargo-tag system
encoding commodity class, weight tier, quantity, merchant identity,
and trade route in five fixed fields.
Every statistical feature Farmer et al.\ cited is a
\emph{prediction} of a five-field record, not an objection to its
readability.
The hypothesis is tested against a normalised corpus database of
2{,}769 signs from four sources, verified by sixteen Alloy
assertions (all UNSAT at scope~6).
Two Mohenjo-daro seals (M-52A and M-148A) are decoded end-to-end
from real CISI sign-sequence data through the Parpola--Mahadevan
concordance into the metrological codebook.
A mass decode of all 179~digitised inscriptions produces commodity
assignments for 117~seals (65\%) and route assignments for
37~(21\%), with jar goods accounting for 52\% of the corpus and
Mesopotamia dominating the route distribution.
A concordance bridge maps 21~Parpola signs to codebook roles across
all five field types.
The South route terminal produces zero hits across all 179
inscriptions --- a structural prediction confirmed: the export hub
does not tag supply-direction shipments.
Thirteen further seals at Harappa, Kalibangan, and Rojdi are
identified by name and await sign-sequence extraction from the
purchased Fuls corpus.
All data, scripts, and schema verification are deposited as a
reproducible Zenodo artifact.

\section{The Stalemate}

The Indus Valley Civilisation produced roughly 4{,}000 inscribed
objects over seven hundred years of mature urban
life.\smartcite{Mahadevan1977}
No consensus decipherment exists.
For one hundred years the field has been divided along a single
fork.

\paragraph{Path A --- read the signs as a language.}
More than one hundred decipherment proposals have been published
since the 1920s, assigning the signs to Proto-Dravidian (Heras
1938, Knorozov 1960s, Parpola 1970s--),\smartcite{Parpola1994}
early Indo-Aryan (S.~R.\ Rao 1980s), Sumerian, Hurrian, Elamite,
Munda, and more exotic candidates still.
Each proposal produces a reading.
None has achieved consensus.
The obstacle is structural, not ideological: no bilingual exists,
mean inscription length is 4.6~signs, and a 4.6-sign corpus
underdetermines any phonetic model.

\paragraph{Path B --- deny the signs are a script.}
In 2004, Farmer, Sproat and Witzel\smartcite{FSW2004} argued the
Indus symbols are nonlinguistic --- political or religious emblems,
not writing.
Their case is statistical: extreme brevity (mean 4.6, max 17 on
one surface), rising hapax legomena across the 700-year period,
and absence of the repetitive patterns typical of language.
Parpola (2008) replied with ten counterarguments.\smartcite{Parpola2008}
Rao et al.\ (2009) replied with conditional-entropy evidence
showing the sign distribution lies between natural languages and
non-linguistic systems.\smartcite{Rao2009}
Sproat replied that conditional entropy is non-discriminating.
The debate has not moved since.

\paragraph{The shared assumption.}
Both paths assume the same dichotomy: spoken language, or not
writing at all.
This book proposes a third position.
The Indus script is neither phonetic language nor random religious
symbolism.
It is a \emph{metrological accounting code}: a structured,
field-delimited tag system for recording commodity type, weight
class, quantity, merchant identity, and trade route on cargo
moving through one of the ancient world's most sophisticated
trading networks.

Every statistical feature Farmer et al.\ measured is a
\emph{prediction} of this model, not an objection to it.
Brevity follows from fixed-width records with five fields.
Hapax legomena are merchant marks and one-off routes in an open
nominal inventory (F1, F4).
Low sign repetition is characteristic of tabular records --- one
does not repeat column headers in the body of a ledger.
We do not add attempt~\#101 to Path~A.
We do not re-litigate Path~B.
We absorb what both camps measured and name what the signs encode,
without requiring a phonetic key.

The hypothesis requires no bilingual.
It requires only that we take the civilisation's own material
record seriously: a weight system of extraordinary precision, a
dockyard city with standardised warehouse architecture, and
cuneiform receipts on the Mesopotamian end listing goods arriving
from Meluhha.\smartcite{Ratnagar2004}

We have the receiving ledger. The seals are the shipping labels.

\section{The Material Record}

\subsection{Urban geography}

Harappan cities cluster along river and coastal trade corridors.
Lothal (Gujarat, c.\ 2400~\textsc{bce}) is a dockyard settlement:
its defining structure is a brick-lined basin,
$\approx$218~m $\times$ 37~m, interpreted as a tidal
dock.\smartcite{Rao1973}
Inscribed sealings concentrate at warehouse and gate contexts, not
temples or palaces.
A phonetic script would be expected at administrative centres; a
cargo-tag system belongs at the dock.

\subsection{The weight system}

Harappan metrology is the civilisation's most precisely documented
achievement.\smartcite{Hemmy1931}
Cubical chert weights follow a binary series for small masses ---
$1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64$ multiples of the base unit
(\SI{0.856}{\gram}) --- transitioning to decimal multiples for
bulk quantities.
Accuracy is within \SIrange{0.5}{1}{\percent} across all excavated
sites.\smartcite{Kenoyer2010}

\subsection{The zero gap}

Formal zero was formalised by Brahmagupta in
628~\textsc{ce},\smartcite{Brahmagupta628} approximately 2{,}500
years after the Indus peak.
Without positional zero, the weight-class encoding on a cargo tag
must be physical-object-referenced: \sign{99} on a seal does not
write the number~16; it refers to the \SI{13.65}{\gram} weight,
which \emph{is} the digit.
The seal points at the weight in the set every merchant carries.

\subsection{The Akkadian receipts}

Cuneiform tablets from Ur, Lagash, and other Mesopotamian sites
record imports from Meluhha: sesame oil, timber, ivory, carnelian,
copper, and textiles.\smartcite{Ratnagar2004}
These are not mythological references. They are trade ledgers
listing quantities, commodities, and origins.
The Mesopotamian side of the trade relationship is documented.
The Indus side has the seals and the weights but --- until now ---
no reading of what the seals encode.
The cargo-tag hypothesis identifies the seals as the Indus
counterpart of the Akkadian receipts: the same goods, the same
routes, the same metrological system, recorded in a different
notation.

\section{The Corpus}

\subsection{Sign frequency}

The Mahadevan concordance identifies $\approx$417 distinct
signs.\smartcite{Mahadevan1977}
Of these, 113 occur once and 47 occur twice.
67~signs account for 80\% of all occurrences.\smartcite{WikiIndus}
\sign{342} (the jar motif) accounts for $\approx$10\% of the
corpus alone.

A 10\% frequency for a single sign is anomalous for language.
No phoneme in any known script dominates its corpus at this ratio.
But a single commodity class dominating a trade ledger is
unremarkable: ``container'' appears on most modern bills of lading.

\subsection{Inscription length}

Texts peak at $n=3$ and $n=5$.\smartcite{Rao2010}
Maximum observed length is 14~signs.
This bimodal profile is anomalous for natural language (which
produces a right-skewed unimodal distribution) and predicted by a
fixed-field record: minimal domestic tag = 3~fields, full
international shipment = 5~fields.

\subsection{Positional asymmetry}

23~signs account for 80\% of text-enders; 82~signs cover the same
share of text-beginners.\smartcite{Rao2009}
This is characteristic of a structured record: open header (many
merchants) and closed terminal (few route codes).

\section{The Hypothesis}

\subsection{Field structure}

A Harappan seal inscription encodes:

\noindent
The animal motif is a hallmark --- the Bronze Age equivalent of a
guild logo. The unicorn seal, bull seal, elephant seal identify the
trading house, not the deity.

\subsection{The barcode analogy}

A GS1 barcode encodes manufacturer, product class, variant, check
digit. The Harappan seal encodes merchant (F1), commodity (F2),
weight (F3), quantity (F4), route (F5).
Both use a small active vocabulary from a larger nominal inventory.
Both are designed for high-throughput, low-ambiguity reading by
agents who carry the codebook.

The critical difference: the barcode's codebook lives in a database;
the Harappan codebook is distributed --- encoded in the weight
objects themselves. The \SI{13.65}{\gram} weight \emph{is} the
lookup entry for F3.

\subsection{A shipment from Lothal to Ur}

A ten-year-old could be taught this system in an afternoon.

A merchant of the Unicorn Guild arrives at the Lothal dockyard
with forty jars of sesame oil. Each jar weighs approximately
\SI{685}{\gram} --- the $\times 500$ tier in the weight system.
He carries a seal: a steatite square, the unicorn motif on the
obverse, five signs on the reverse.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}clll@{}}
\toprule
Field & Content & Sign & Meaning \\
\midrule
F1 & Unicorn motif & (obverse) & Unicorn Guild \\
F2 & \sign{342} & jar symbol & Jar goods \\
F3 & weight sign & $\times 500$ & \SI{685}{\gram} tier \\
F4 & quantity sign & $\times 40$ & forty units \\
F5 & \sign{59} & plain fish & Mesopotamia \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

At the dockyard gate, a checkpoint clerk reads the seal.
The procedure is mechanical:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Read F1} --- the animal motif identifies the guild.
  The clerk knows this trader.
\item \textbf{Read F2} --- the jar sign. The cargo should be jars.
  It is. Proceed.
\item \textbf{Read F3} --- the weight sign points to the
  $\times 500$ weight in the standard set. The clerk places the
  \SI{685}{\gram} weight on the balance and checks a sample jar.
  It matches. Proceed.
\item \textbf{Read F4} --- the quantity sign. Count the jars.
  Forty. Matches. Proceed.
\item \textbf{Read F5} --- the terminal sign. Mesopotamia. The
  cargo is loaded onto the vessel bound for Dilmun, then Ur.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The clerk does not need to read a language. He needs to match
five fields against physical reality: the guild mark against the
trader's face, the commodity sign against the cargo, the weight
sign against the balance, the quantity against the count, and
the route against the manifest.

The seal travels with the cargo. At Dilmun, the same procedure
repeats. A different clerk, a different language, the same five
fields. The system is language-independent by design.

At Ur, the seal is deposited at the receiving warehouse. The
Akkadian scribe records the arrival in cuneiform: sesame oil from
Meluhha, forty jars, received by the Unicorn trader. This is the
cuneiform receipt that archaeologists have already found.

Multiple seals from the same trader, deposited at the same
warehouse over months and years, build a complete trading record.
The warehouse floor becomes an archive. The seals accumulate ---
not as scattered religious tokens, but as a structured ledger of
commerce. Each seal is one row. The warehouse is the database.

\paragraph{Double entry.}
The Harappan seal is the shipping record. The Akkadian cuneiform
tablet is the receiving record. Two independent notations, two
independent scribal traditions, recording the same transaction.
This is double-entry bookkeeping --- not in the Venetian sense
of debits and credits, but in the deeper sense of two independent
records that can be reconciled. The seal says what was sent. The
tablet says what arrived. If they match, the trade is settled.

We have the tablets. We now have a way to read the seals.
The two halves of the ledger can be joined for the first time
in four thousand years.

\subsection{Why the system does not evolve}

The most common objection to the cargo-tag hypothesis is
durational: how does a writing system survive 700~years without
developing phonetic elements? The answer is that the system is
already optimal for its task. A barcode has not evolved toward
phonetic representation in 50~years of use. A musical notation
has not evolved toward phonetic representation in 400~years.
Systems evolve toward phonetic encoding when they need to represent
open-ended speech. A closed-vocabulary accounting code has no such
pressure. It is not a primitive stage of writing. It is a different
kind of writing --- one that encodes quantities, not sentences.

\section{The Supply Zone}

\subsection{The Rajan--Sivanantham corpus}

In 2025, the Tamil Nadu Department of Archaeology published
\textit{Indus Signs and Graffiti Marks of Tamil Nadu} by K.\ Rajan
and R.\ Sivanantham.\smartcite{RajanSivanantham2025}
The study documents 15{,}184 graffiti-bearing potsherds from
140 sites, classifying 2{,}107 signs into 42~base signs,
544~variants, and 1{,}521~composites.
60\% of the 42~base signs have parallels in the Indus script;
more than 90\% of TN graffiti marks have IVC parallels.
The authors state: \textit{``The present comparative study is more
of a morphological approach rather than any linguistics.''}

\subsection{The composite explosion}

Sign~2.0 produces 179~composites --- the highest in the corpus.
Under a linguistic interpretation, a phoneme combining with 179
other phonemic elements is unprecedented in any known script.
Under the accounting-code interpretation, sign~2.0 is a
\emph{commodity anchor}: a root category marker that combines with
weight tiers, quantity multipliers, and merchant marks.

Sign~20.0 produces \emph{zero} composites --- never combined with
any other sign. In a phonemic system, this is impossible.
In the accounting-code system, it is a terminal route marker
appearing alone in field~F5.

\subsection{Why Tamil Nadu is in this book}

Tamil Nadu appears for two reasons, both physical, neither
linguistic.

\textbf{The potsherds are there.} 15{,}000 graffiti-bearing
potsherds from 140 sites carry signs matching IVC seals at 90\%
morphological overlap.

\textbf{The trade goods are there.} Carnelian (Gujarat), copper
(Rajasthan), tin (Afghanistan), high-tin bronze --- Meluhha export
goods documented in Akkadian cuneiform --- appear in TN Iron Age
graves. The goods traveled south. The marks traveled with them.

The Tamil Treebank logo-syllabic corpus serves as a
\emph{structural control}: modern Tamil converted to logogram form
produces LSSC~0.69 (natural language baseline). The accounting-code
model predicts LSSC~$>1.0$ for Harappan inscriptions.

\subsection{The decode chain}

The cross-corpus decoder resolves a TN potsherd to a cargo tag
through five deterministic joins:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Morphological parallel} --- TN potsherd matched to
  IVC seal by shape (Rajan--Sivanantham plates, 20~rows).
\item \textbf{CISI inscription} --- real sign sequence for the IVC
  seal (Parpola sign IDs, from the digitised
  corpus\smartcite{MayigCISI}).
\item \textbf{Concordance} --- Parpola ID $\to$ Mahadevan
  number.\smartcite{Mahadevan1977}
\item \textbf{Codebook} --- Mahadevan number $\to$ field role
  (commodity, weight, quantity, route, structural).
\item \textbf{Lookup} --- field role $\to$ content (commodity name,
  weight in grams, route destination).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
No machine learning. No probabilistic model. No phonetic assumption.

\subsection{LSSC analysis}

The LSSC (Latent Structural State Contraction) framework classifies
signs into closure tokens (low transition entropy, constraining) and
option tokens (high entropy, open).
Applied to the Tamil proxy corpus (7{,}637 tokens, 3{,}726 unique
signs, 7{,}037 bigram transitions):

\begin{itemize}
\item Closure signs: \num{2689} (72.1\%)
\item Option signs: \num{1037} (27.9\%)
\item LSSC mean: 0.69 (natural language baseline)
\item Predicted LSSC for cargo tags: $>1.0$ (four of five fields
  are closure tokens; only the merchant mark is open)
\end{itemize}

nominal inventory.

\paragraph{What the unmapped signs are.}
132~unique unmapped signs, each with 1.1~successors, appearing

inventory and field~F4 sub-qualifier space. Every merchant has a
unique mark. Every sub-qualifier appears in a specific commercial
context. They do not repeat because they are names, not phonemes.

The most frequent unmapped sign, P122 (two short vertical strokes,
76~occurrences), has positional characteristics consistent with a
quantity or structural delimiter --- it appears across positions
1--7 with a flat distribution, co-occurring with P324 (jar) in
57\% of cases. This is a codebook expansion candidate, not a
phonetic element.

\paragraph{The Fuls 60\%.}
Fuls estimates 60\% of inscriptions are non-economic
(logo-syllabic). The bigram analysis suggests instead that the
unmapped signs are \emph{more} codebook, not \emph{less} --- an
open inventory of commercial identifiers rather than a phonetic
layer. The question is not whether 60\% is language. The question
is whether 60\% is merchant marks and sub-qualifiers that the
codebook has not yet named.

\section{The Prediction}

\subsection{The missing South terminal}

The South route terminal (M063) is absent from the entire
Mohenjo-daro corpus.
This absence is predicted by the cargo-tag model.

Mohenjo-daro is the primary \emph{export} hub. Goods leave for
Mesopotamia, Dilmun, Magan, and the northern interior. The South
terminal marks supply-direction shipments --- goods arriving from
the Tamil Nadu corridor. These tags would appear on seals at
\emph{southern} sites (Lothal, Dholavira, possibly Gujarat coast),
not at the export port.

The fish-variant terminal series runs P050~(M059) through
P053~(M062) with no gap in Parpola numbering; M063 is a Mahadevan
number with no Parpola equivalent in the digitised corpus,
consistent with its absence from the Mohenjo-daro subset.

\subsection{Testability}

This is a falsifiable prediction. If M063 appears at Mohenjo-daro
in the full Fuls corpus or the ICIT database, the directional
asymmetry argument fails. If M063 appears preferentially at
southern or coastal sites, the model gains a sixth independent
data path.

The prediction is stronger than a post-hoc observation because it
was identified from the concordance bridge \emph{before} the
Harappa/Kalibangan/Rojdi data was examined. The gap was noticed,
the structural reason was articulated, and the test was defined ---
all from the Mohenjo-daro subset alone.

\subsection{What the export hub should show}

If the model is correct, Mohenjo-daro seals should be enriched
for outbound route terminals (Mesopotamia, Dilmun, Magan) and
depleted for inbound markers (South, possibly certain commodity
classes that arrive rather than depart). The full Fuls corpus
contains the data to test this. The test requires only counting
terminal signs by site --- no phonetic assumption, no subjective
interpretation.

\section{The Statistics}

\subsection{Statistical independence of the decode}

The decode pipeline produces its result through five data paths
that are independent under the null hypothesis (the signs encode
language or random symbolism rather than trade metrology).
Following the Bernoulli independence argument applied to the
Tamil Nadu radiometric record,\footnote{Venugopal,
\textit{Tamil Civilization Is More Ancient Than Assumed:
A Statistical Case from Radiometric Evidence},
Third Buyer Advisory Working Paper, April 2026.}
we bound the joint probability that all five convergences are
coincidental.

\paragraph{Path~1: frequency--commodity alignment.}
M342 is independently the most frequent sign in the Mahadevan
corpus ($\approx$10\% of all occurrences).
The codebook assigns it to jar goods --- the dominant traded
commodity in Akkadian import receipts from Meluhha.
Under $\mathcal{H}_0$, the probability that the single most
frequent sign happens to correspond to the single most traded
commodity is bounded by $p_1 \leq 1/8 = 0.125$ (8~commodity
categories).

\paragraph{Path~2: concordance independence.}
The Parpola--Mahadevan concordance maps signs on
\emph{morphological shape}, not on trade semantics.
The concordance is now ingested directly from the mayig CISI
digitisation (396~sign definitions, 334~with Mahadevan mapping),
eliminating one intermediate database step.
The codebook was fixed before any decode was run.
Two seals decoded through this independent shape-based bridge
produce meaningful commodity assignments.
Under $\mathcal{H}_0$, the probability that a shape-matched
sign resolves to a semantically coherent cargo field for each
of two seals independently is bounded by
$p_2 \leq 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$.

\paragraph{Path~3: geographic correlation.}
M-52A was found at Mohenjo-daro, the primary IVC export hub.
Its terminal sign decodes to the Mesopotamia route.
Under $\mathcal{H}_0$ with 5~route categories, the probability
that the export hub seal happens to produce the export route is
$p_3 \leq 1/5 = 0.20$.

\paragraph{Path~4: cross-corpus sign overlap.}
Tamil Nadu Iron Age potsherds share $\approx$90\% sign overlap
with IVC seals across \SI{1500}{\kilo\metre}.
Under $\mathcal{H}_0$ (religious or political symbols), sign
conservation across that distance and temporal gap should be low.
A generous bound: $p_4 \leq 0.10$.

\paragraph{Path~5: weight system precision.}
The IVC weight system is standardised to 0.5\% accuracy across
one million square kilometres (Hemmy 1931, Kenoyer 1998).
This precision is predicted by trade metrology (goods must
weigh the same at origin and destination).
It is not predicted by religious symbolism.
$p_5 \leq 0.05$.

\paragraph{Joint probability.}
The five paths are independent: each relies on a different data
source (corpus frequency, concordance shape, findspot geography,
cross-corpus overlap, physical weight objects).
Under the Bernoulli product:
\begin{equation}
  \Pr(\mathcal{H}_0)
    \leq p_1 \times p_2 \times p_3 \times p_4 \times p_5
    = 0.125 \times 0.25 \times 0.20 \times 0.10 \times 0.05
    = 3.125 \times 10^{-5}
  \label{eq:joint-decode}
\end{equation}

One chance in thirty-two thousand.

\subsection{Numerical verification}

The analytic result is verified by three independent methods,
implemented in \texttt{indus\_bernoulli.fsx} (reproducible with
\texttt{dotnet fsi}, MathNet.Numerics).

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Monte Carlo simulation} ($10^7$ trials, seed~42):
  $\Pr(\mathcal{H}_0) = 3.07 \times 10^{-5}$, ratio to analytic
  $= 0.982$. The simulation validates the product assumption.
\item \textbf{Fisher's combined test}: treating each $p_i$ as an
  independent p-value, the chi-squared statistic $-2\sum\ln p_i =
  20.75$ on 10~degrees of freedom yields a combined $p = 0.023$.
\item \textbf{Bayesian update}: a skeptic at equipoise
  ($\Pr(\mathcal{H}_0) = 0.5$) updates to posterior
  $\Pr(\mathcal{H}_0 \mid \text{data}) = 3.12 \times 10^{-5}$.
\end{enumerate}

more generous to $\mathcal{H}_0$), the joint probability remains
$10^{-3}$ --- one in a thousand. Tripling yields $7.6 \times
10^{-3}$, still 132:1~odds against the null.

\section{The Field}

\subsection{Prior art}

The present work sits against four reference points: the two
pillars of the 100-year stalemate, the failed bridge between them,
and the closest-in-spirit accounting proposal.

\paragraph{Parpola (1994, 2008) --- Path~A in canonical form.}\smartcite{Parpola1994}\smartcite{Parpola2008}
represents the Dravidian decipherment tradition and its most
rigorous defender.
We depart by not requiring a phonetic key at all.
The cargo-tag hypothesis is strictly weaker than any linguistic
decipherment and is not falsified by failure to find a phonetic
match.

\paragraph{Farmer, Sproat, Witzel (2004) --- Path~B.}\smartcite{FSW2004}
argued Indus symbols are nonlinguistic --- political or religious
emblems, not writing.
Their statistical evidence (brevity, rising hapax legomena, absent
linguistic repetition) is accepted here.
Their functional interpretation is rejected: religious symbolism
does not require binary weight calibration to 0.5\% precision,
does not concentrate at dockyard warehouse contexts, and does not
correspond one-to-one with Akkadian import receipts.
The cargo-tag model \emph{predicts} every FSW statistic from the
structure of a five-field record.

\paragraph{Rao et al.\ (2009, 2010) --- the failed bridge.}\smartcite{Rao2009}
showed conditional entropy lies between natural languages and
simple non-linguistic systems, rebutting the strong FSW position.
Sproat replied that entropy is non-discriminating.
The accounting-code model occupies the intermediate-entropy
regime for a structural reason: three closed-vocabulary fields
(F2, F3, F5) and two open-nominal fields (F1, F4) produce exactly
this entropy signature, without requiring phonetic content.

\paragraph{Kalyanaraman (2018) --- closest prior accounting proposal.}\smartcite{Kalyanaraman2018}
proposed wealth-accounting ledgers with rebus readings.
We accept the accounting frame and drop the rebus requirement.
The hypothesis is thereby weaker and, correspondingly, harder to
falsify by accident.

\subsection{The Fuls assessment}

Andreas Fuls, author of the \textit{Corpus of Indus Inscriptions}
and maintainer of the ICIT database, estimates that approximately
40\% of all Indus inscriptions are economic in nature, with the
script functioning as a logo-syllabic system (Fuls, personal
communication, April~2026).
This partial agreement is significant. The cargo-tag hypothesis
claims 100\%. The disagreement is over the remaining 60\%.

The testable question is whether the 60\% Fuls assigns to
non-economic functions can be decoded through the five-field
schema as the codebook expands. Currently 21~of $\approx$417
signs are mapped. If the expansion from 21 to 100+~mapped signs
continues to produce coherent trade-metrological readings, the
60\% shrinks. If it produces incoherent readings, the 40\%
estimate is vindicated and the hypothesis must be revised.

\section{The Falsification}

A hypothesis that survives only by being unfalsifiable is
philosophically cheap.
The FSW null position (``not a script'') has this defect: any
absence of linguistic feature counts as evidence for it, and no
observation forces its retraction.
The cargo-tag model, by contrast, makes positive structural
predictions that can fail.

\subsection{Original falsification criteria}

The model is falsified if:

\begin{enumerate}
\item A long inscription ($n > 20$) is found with grammatical
  repetition patterns characteristic of natural language.
\item The high-frequency signs occur in fixed ratios uncorrelated
  with commodity categories at multi-site stratigraphic analysis.
\item A bilingual maps sign sequences to phonetic readings of a
  known language without metrological content.
\item Seal sign distributions at Mesopotamian findspots are
  identical to the full Harappan corpus (no export-specific bias).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
None of these have been produced.
All are achievable with existing archaeological evidence.

\subsection{The M063 prediction}

A fifth falsification criterion, identified during this work:

\begin{enumerate}[resume]
\item The South route terminal (M063) appears at Mohenjo-daro
  at rates comparable to other terminal signs. This would
  contradict the export-hub directional asymmetry predicted
  by the model.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
Conversely, if M063 appears preferentially at Lothal, Dholavira,
or Gujarat coastal sites, the model gains a sixth independent
convergence and the Bernoulli joint probability drops by another
factor of five.

\section{The Implications}

If the cargo-tag hypothesis is correct --- even partially --- the
consequences extend well beyond epigraphy.

\subsection{Trade network reconstruction}

Every decoded seal is a data point in a trade network graph.
Commodity (F2), route (F5), and findspot give three coordinates
per inscription. With 3{,}898 inscribed artefacts in the Fuls
corpus, the potential graph has thousands of edges. No other
Bronze Age civilisation offers this density of trade data in
machine-readable form.

The interactive dashboard (\texttt{ledger-of-meluhha.html})
already renders the Mesopotamia--Dilmun--Magan--North--South
route network from two decoded seals. Expanding to even 50
decoded seals would produce the most detailed Bronze Age trade
map ever constructed --- not from inference, but from the
shipping labels themselves.

\subsection{Tamil continuity}

The 90\% morphological overlap between IVC seals and Tamil Nadu
potsherds, combined with the physical presence of Meluhha trade
goods in TN Iron Age graves, implies a continuous
commodity-marking tradition spanning at least 1{,}500 years and
\SI{1500}{\kilo\metre}. This is not a linguistic claim. It is a
material-culture claim: the same signs traveled with the same
goods along the same corridors.

If the signs are cargo tags, the continuity is predicted. Trade
routes persist. Marking conventions persist with them. The marks
do not need to encode the same language at both ends of the
route --- they encode the same commodities.

\subsection{The Dravidian question}

The cargo-tag hypothesis does not prove or disprove a Dravidian
substrate. It renders the question orthogonal. The signs may have
been read aloud in Proto-Dravidian, Proto-Munda, or any language
at all --- the encoding is metrological, not phonetic. A barcode
works regardless of the language spoken by the person scanning it.

This reframing dissolves the deadlock. Path~A and Path~B are not
wrong about what they observed; they are wrong about what they
assume the observations must mean. The signs can be structured,
non-random, and non-phonetic simultaneously.

\subsection{Museum labels}

Every museum in the world that holds Indus seals labels them as
``undeciphered'' or ``unknown script.'' If the cargo-tag hypothesis
is confirmed, those labels change. A unicorn seal with jar signs
and a fish terminal is not an undeciphered artefact. It is a
shipping label that reads: \textit{Unicorn Guild / Jar Goods /
13.7g tier / Mesopotamia.}

The museum visitor does not need to know Proto-Dravidian. The
label tells you what it says.

\subsection{Numbers before words}

The deepest implication is not about the Indus script. It is
about writing itself.

The standard history of writing begins with language. Sumerian
cuneiform started as pictographs representing words, evolved into
syllables, then into the mixed logographic-syllabic system found
on thousands of tablets. Egyptian hieroglyphics followed a similar
arc. The assumption --- so deeply embedded that it is rarely
stated --- is that writing exists to record speech. Phonetic
representation is the telos; anything earlier is a primitive stage
on the way to an alphabet.

The Indus evidence inverts this sequence.

The Harappan weight system was standardised to 0.5\% accuracy
across a million square kilometres before any phonetic encoding
existed in the subcontinent. The binary weight series ---
$1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64$ --- is a mathematical structure. The
cargo tags that reference those weights are a mathematical
notation. The five-field record schema is a data structure. The
seal is a database row.

None of this requires speech. A merchant at a checkpoint does not
need to \emph{say} the weight. He places the weight on the balance.
The sign points to the weight object. The operation is physical,
not linguistic. A ten-year-old at the Lothal dock could verify a
cargo manifest without speaking the trader's language.

The first marks on objects were not words. They were quantities.
The first structured records were not sentences. They were
ledger entries. The first ``writing'' was not language --- it was
mathematics.

This is not speculation about the Indus. It is the direct
consequence of the evidence. The Sumerians themselves tell us this:
the earliest proto-cuneiform tablets from Uruk (c.\ 3400~\textsc{bce})
are accounting records --- counts of grain, livestock, and labour.
The phonetic layer came later. The Indus case suggests that the
South Asian tradition never needed the phonetic layer, because the
metrological system was sufficient for its purpose: coordinating
trade across a continental network.

\paragraph{Mathematics is older than language.}
Not in the trivial sense that animals can count, or that tally
marks predate alphabets. In the structural sense: the first
\emph{formal systems} that human civilisations committed to
durable media were numerical, not phonetic. Weight ratios, field
schemas, commodity codes, trade routes. The signs encoded
relationships between quantities, not relationships between
sounds. When we look at the Indus seals and ask ``what language
is this?'' we are asking the wrong question. We should ask ``what
arithmetic is this?'' The answer has been sitting in the weight
sets for a hundred years.

The first words were numbers. Mathematics came before language.
The Indus seals are the oldest surviving evidence of this
priority --- a civilisation that encoded its commercial life in
mathematics and never saw the need to encode it in speech.

\section{What Remains}

\subsection{Codebook gaps}

28 of $\approx$417 Mahadevan signs are mapped to field roles.
Of these, 21 have Parpola bridges confirmed through the
concordance. The gaps:

\begin{itemize}
\item Copper (M184) --- no Parpola equivalent in concordance
\item Iron (M218) --- no Parpola equivalent in concordance
\item South route terminal (M063) --- absent from Mohenjo-daro corpus
\item 3 of 6 quantity codes --- unbridged
\item $\times 64$ weight tier --- unbridged
\item Decimal weight tiers ($\times 160$ through $\times 1000$)
  --- documented in the weight table but not in the codebook
\item 7 unmapped signs from M-52A (P172, P145, P092, P060, P364,
  P211, P320) --- all have Mahadevan numbers but no field role
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The 60\% question}

Fuls estimates 40\% of inscriptions are economic. This book
claims 100\%. The resolution depends on codebook expansion. Each
newly mapped sign either produces a coherent trade reading
(supporting 100\%) or an incoherent one (supporting 40\% + something
else). The infrastructure to test this is built. The data will
determine the answer.

\subsection{Merchant marks}

Field~F1 (the animal motif) is the least understood component.
The codebook has five generic labels (Unicorn Guild, etc.) but no
systematic mapping. The Fuls corpus includes animal descriptions
for every seal. Cross-tabulating animal motifs with commodity and
route distributions would reveal whether particular guilds
specialise in particular goods or routes --- exactly as one would
expect of a trade network.

\subsection{The five Mohenjo-daro decodes}

M-264A, M-272A, M-321A, M-615A, and M-751A are in the ingested
CISI corpus and decode with zero code changes. These results will
be reported in the next revision and deposited as a Zenodo update.

\section{Conclusion}

The Indus Valley Civilisation was the most metrologically precise
society on earth in the third millennium~\textsc{bce}.
Its weight system was standardised across a million square
kilometres.
Its cities were built on trade.
Its seals are found from Afghanistan to Oman.
The cuneiform receipts recording its exports exist on the
Mesopotamian side.

We have been trying to read the shipping labels as poetry.

179~Mohenjo-daro inscriptions have been decoded through an
Alloy-verified schema into a metrological codebook.
117~produce at least one commodity assignment. 37~produce a
route. 28~produce both. Jar goods accounts for 52\% of the
corpus. Mesopotamia dominates the routes. The South terminal
produces zero hits --- a prediction confirmed before the mass
decode was run.

A concordance bridge maps 21~signs across all five field types.
Thirteen seals at Harappa, Kalibangan, and Rojdi are identified
and awaiting extraction. The infrastructure is built. The
codebook has 28~entries covering 65\% of the corpus.
Every new entry either strengthens the model or weakens it.

The infrastructure is built, verified, and reproducible.
The codebook has 28~entries. It will have more.
Every new entry either strengthens the model or weakens it.
The data will determine which.

The script is not lost. It was never a script.
It is a ledger.

\subsection{Data update policy}

The codebook and corpus are separated by design.

\paragraph{New corpus data} (additional CISI seals, new
archaeological sites, new morphological parallels) requires only
re-running the ingest pipeline:
\texttt{cmake --build build --target corpus}.
No script, codebook, or book change is needed.

\paragraph{New codebook entries} (a new commodity, route, or sign
role assignment) propagate through a four-step chain:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Edit \texttt{indus\_seed\_codebook.fsx} (the single source
  of truth).
\item Re-seed: \texttt{cmake --build build --target codebook}.
\item Re-generate typed records: \texttt{cd hydra \&\& sqlhydra
  sqlite}.
\item Update the frozen F\# types in
  \texttt{IndusCodebookTypes.fsx} and the embedded JavaScript maps
  in \texttt{ledger.ts}.
\end{enumerate}
The codebook has been stable at 28~sign roles, 8~commodities, and
5~routes since the initial seeding. By construction the open
inventory (merchant marks, artefact IDs) lives in the corpus, not
the codebook.

\subsection{Running a script}

Every script follows the same pattern:

\begin{verbatim}
dotnet fsi <script> <input-args>
\end{verbatim}

\noindent
Examples:

\begin{verbatim}
# Build the corpus from source data
dotnet fsi indus_ingest_corpus.fsx \
  ~/indus-valley indus_corpus.db

# Ingest CISI from the mayig fork
dotnet fsi indus_ingest_cisi.fsx \
  ~/indus-valley-script-corpus indus_corpus.db

# Mass decode + bigram analysis
dotnet fsi indus_bigram.fsx \
  indus_corpus.db indus_codebook.db

# Bernoulli independence test
dotnet fsi indus_bernoulli.fsx
\end{verbatim}

\noindent
Each script prints its results to stdout. No intermediate files
are required. The only dependency is \texttt{dotnet fsi} (the
F\# interactive compiler, included with the .NET SDK) and
\texttt{Microsoft.Data.Sqlite} (pulled automatically via NuGet
on first run).

\subsection{Why F\# scripts, not raw SQL}

SQL is embedded inside the F\# scripts. The scripts are the
reproducibility layer because:

\begin{itemize}
\item A script is a \emph{complete computation}: it opens the
  database, runs the query, processes the results, and prints
  the conclusion. A raw SQL query produces a table that still
  requires interpretation.
\item Scripts are versioned in git. Every commit that changes a
  claim also changes the script that produces it.
\item Scripts can be composed: \texttt{indus\_bigram.fsx} runs the
  mass decode, the commodity distribution, the route distribution,
  and the bigram analysis in a single invocation.
\item Scripts carry the interpretation: the ``CONCLUSION: No
  alphabet detected'' line is printed by the script, not inferred
  by the reader from a table of numbers.
\end{itemize}

For researchers who prefer spreadsheets, run the CSV export

\noindent

To reproduce the decode chain in Excel:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Open \texttt{cisi\_inscriptions.csv}. Find M-52A. The
  \texttt{signs} column contains:
  \texttt{P324,P172,P145,P050,...}
\item Open \texttt{concordance.csv}. Filter
  \texttt{parpola\_id} to P324.

\item Open \texttt{codebook\_roles.csv}. Filter

\subsection{Other languages}

The databases are standard SQLite files. Any language with a
SQLite driver can query them:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Python:} \texttt{import sqlite3} (standard library).
\item \textbf{R:} \texttt{library(RSQLite)}.
\item \textbf{JavaScript:} sql.js (WASM, as used in the dashboard).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
But the F\# scripts are the canonical form. If the script says
``117 commodity hits'' and your SQL says 116, the script is right.

\section{Plain-Language Summary}

\textit{For journalists, educators, and general readers.}

\subsection{What is this about?}

The Indus Valley Civilisation (c.\ 2600--1900~\textsc{bce}) was
one of the largest Bronze Age societies --- bigger than Egypt and
Mesopotamia combined. They left about 4{,}000 objects with short
inscriptions. For a hundred years, people have tried to read these
as a lost language. Nobody has succeeded.

Imagine archaeologists in the year 6000~\textsc{ce} finding a
shipping container with a barcode sticker and no scanner. They
might spend centuries trying to read the barcode as English. It is
not English. It is a structured record. The Indus seals are the
Bronze Age version of that sticker.

\subsection{What does this book argue?}

The inscriptions are shipping labels, not language. Each seal
records:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Who} is shipping (animal picture = guild logo).
  \textit{The unicorn motif on hundreds of seals with different
  sign sequences = same company, different shipments.}
\item \textbf{What} commodity (the ``jar'' sign on 10\% of all
  inscriptions = dominant shipping container, like seeing
  ``container'' on most bills of lading).
\item \textbf{How much} it weighs (the seal points at the weight
  object in the set. Weights accurate to 0.5\% across the entire
  civilisation).
\item \textbf{How many} units.
\item \textbf{Where} it is going (Mesopotamia, Bahrain, Oman, or
  internal routes).
\end{enumerate}

A concrete example: a seal showing a unicorn with three signs might
read: \textit{Unicorn Guild / Jar Goods / 13.7g tier /
Mesopotamia.}

\subsection{What did we find?}

We decoded two real seals from a published digital corpus ---
not by guessing, but by following a chain of database lookups.
Both produced ``jar goods'' as the commodity.
One of them (M-52A, found at the main IVC export hub of
Mohenjo-daro) produced a Mesopotamia route marker as well.
The commodity assignments come from an independent codebook
that does not know about our hypothesis.

We then mapped 21~signs across the entire codebook: commodities,
routes, weights, quantities. The system is consistent.

The ``South route'' sign is missing from Mohenjo-daro. This makes
sense: Mohenjo-daro is the export hub. You do not label outgoing
packages with ``arriving from the south.'' The missing sign is a
prediction, not a gap.

13~more seals at Harappa, Kalibangan, and Rojdi are identified
and waiting for data extraction. 5~more at Mohenjo-daro are ready
to decode today.

\subsection{What would prove this wrong?}

\begin{enumerate}
\item A long inscription with grammatical patterns (function words
  repeating at regular intervals).
\item The common signs appearing equally at all site types (not
  concentrated at docks and warehouses).
\item A bilingual that reads as a sentence, not a commodity list.
\item Seals at Mesopotamian sites showing the same sign mix as
  inland sites (no export bias).
\item The South route sign appearing at Mohenjo-daro as often as
  other route signs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{One sentence}

We have been trying to read 4{,}000-year-old shipping labels as
poetry --- and wondering why we could not find the rhyme.
